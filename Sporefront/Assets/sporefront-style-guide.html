Mood Board & Style Guide

Sporefront

A slow-paced hex-grid RTS — where ink meets spore

Pen-Drawn Fantasy Cartography · Fungal Kingdoms · Parchment & Ink

01 — Vision

The World on Parchment

Sporefront should feel like unrolling an ancient cartographer's map and discovering that the mushroom kingdoms drawn upon it are alive and moving. The visual language is rooted in hand-drawn fantasy cartography — black ink on aged paper — with fungal entities emerging from the parchment in muted, earthy color washes. Every element should feel drawn, not rendered.

✦
Hand-Drawn, Not Digital
Lines should vary in weight and have subtle imperfections. No perfectly straight edges. Hex borders should feel like they were ruled with a pen, not generated by a computer. Think wobble, not perfection.
✦
Ink on Parchment
The dominant palette is monochrome — dark brown-black ink on warm parchment. Color is used sparingly and deliberately, reserved for entities and important UI elements. Less is more.
✦
Fungal, Not Floral
The organic shapes in this world are mushrooms, mycelium, and spore networks — not flowers and trees. Rounded caps, bulbous stems, branching networks. Shapes feel damp and earthen, not bright and airy.
✦
Slow & Contemplative
The visual pacing matches the gameplay. Animations are gentle and organic — spores drifting, mycelium slowly spreading, ink blooming into newly explored hexes. Nothing flashy or urgent.

03 — The Map

Hex Tiles & Terrain

Each hex tile is a piece of the parchment. Terrain features — mountains, forests, water — are drawn in ink on top of the parchment surface, just as they would be on a Tolkien-style map. The hex grid lines themselves should feel like faint ruled lines, lighter than the terrain ink.

Hex Border Weight
Grid lines at 0.5–1px in Ink Faded (#8A7D6A), noticeably lighter than terrain features. They should guide the eye without competing with the map content. Consider slight opacity variation per line.
Terrain Ink
Mountains, trees, and terrain markers drawn in Ink Dark (#2C2418) at 1–1.5px stroke. Use varied stroke widths and hand-drawn paths — no perfectly geometric shapes. Water features in slightly lighter ink.
Parchment Variation
Different hex tiles can subtly vary in parchment tone to suggest age, wear, or terrain type. Water hexes might be slightly cooler, mountain hexes slightly darker at the edges. Use fractal noise textures for grain.
Fog of War
Unexplored hexes could appear as blank, slightly darker parchment — as if the cartographer hasn't drawn that part of the map yet. Exploration literally draws the world into existence.

04 — Entity Style

Mushroom Entities

The core design question: should mushroom entities be pure ink drawings like the terrain, or should they use color to stand apart? Below are two approaches. The recommended approach uses ink outlines with muted watercolor washes — keeping the hand-drawn feel while giving entities the contrast needed for gameplay clarity.

Option A: Pure Ink
Mushrooms drawn entirely in ink, same style as terrain. Heavier line weight and more detail to distinguish from landscape features.
+ Perfectly cohesive with map aesthetic
+ Simpler to implement consistently
− Hard to distinguish entities from terrain at a glance
− Player ownership / team colors become difficult
Recommended
Recommended
Option B: Ink + Color Wash
Ink outlines with translucent watercolor fills. Entities have color; terrain stays monochrome. Like a cartographer who hand-tinted the important features.
+ Instant visual separation from terrain
+ Natural way to show player/team ownership
+ Still feels hand-drawn and cohesive
− Requires careful color balancing to not feel "digital"

05 — Typography

Fonts & Lettering

A two-tier type system: a display face for region names and titles (the sweeping map labels), and a body face for UI text and gameplay information. Both should feel rooted in the tradition of hand-lettered cartography. Below are recommended pairings using freely available Google Fonts that capture the aesthetic — with notes on premium alternatives.

06 — Design Principles

Rules of the Realm

⬡
Ink Before Color
Every visual element starts as an ink drawing. Color is a secondary layer — a wash applied on top. If you removed all color, the map and entities should still be fully readable in black and white.
⬡
Imperfect by Design
No perfect circles, no ruler-straight lines, no pixel-perfect alignment. Use SVG path randomization, varied stroke widths, and subtle noise. The hand-drawn quality is the brand.
⬡
Color = Life
The monochrome ink map is the "dead" layer — static terrain. Color only appears where living things exist (mushroom entities, mycelium networks, spore clouds). This creates a powerful visual metaphor.
⬡
Reveal, Don't Render
New terrain should feel like being drawn into existence — ink blooming on parchment. Don't pop it in; animate it as if a pen is sketching it in real time. Fog of war = blank parchment.
⬡
Silhouette First
Every mushroom entity type must be identifiable by silhouette alone. Tall/thin, wide/flat, clustered, round — shape language matters more than detail. Players will be zoomed out most of the time.
⬡
The Cartographer's Desk
UI elements outside the map (menus, panels, tooltips) should feel like they're on the cartographer's desk — the same parchment, the same ink. Think hand-written labels, not modern UI buttons.
